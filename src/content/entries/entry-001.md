## Extraction Shooters

I'm very new to the extraction shooter genre so it's taking some getting used to. The initial idea of a competitive extraction shooter is punishing, unforgiving, and demanding with a high barrier of entry (which Bungie absolutely delivers on!) and to be honest, framed in this way a little off-putting. Through discussions with friends (and internal to myself as well), I'm often met with the feeling of "so what?". Why are we doing this? The game loop looks like this:

- Player starts the game and moves through the world gathering resources and interacting with the world (fighting AI, other players, visiting locations and looting)
- If player successfully "extracts" or "exfils" from the instance - they bring everything that they have with them (that they found in the world) into their permanent inventory
- If player does not "extract" or "exfil" from the instance successfully they lose everything that they have currently on them in the instance (the permanent inventory/vault is not touched)
- Rinse and repeat

In this framing, one can imagine (through very careful gameplay and continuous successful exfils) a potentially slow power creep that probably nets a player better gear over many games. This is the best case scenario. But this loop quickly becomes uninteresting. We are gaining better gear but for what? To make things easier? Go to different areas on the map that you previously weren't geared for? (this second point is a little interesting but I think ultimately fails to deliver continuous repeatability). There is nothing else to do but go back in with your better gear and run the risk of potentially losing it. And the loss of gear using this approach is too penalizing to have any interest in "doing it all over again". It's too great of a time investment.

Or maybe a player fails to exfil or gets eliminated every attempt and loses everything. This too would quickly become an uninteresting game loop and dissuade players from continuing to play.

This establishes the upper and lower bounds of my knee-jerk reaction and introduction to the genre. I found that by lightly reframing the way I looked at the game, combined with Bungie's novel approach to addressing these pain points, Marathon in particular can and does really shine and I was able to get past asking myself "What is this for?".

## Breakthrough

At its core, Marathon is a competitive PvP shooter. It isn't about "building up gear".

Through patience and time I think that I had a breakthrough last night. I had to reframe the game from what is described above to - it's a PvP game with a different structure around it. Once I started thinking about it in this way, everything else started to make sense. No different than playing matches of Halo or Overwatch or Destiny or even a BR like PUBG or Apex Legends. A player has to separate themselves from this idea of power grind and attachment to their gear. This may be a difficult mental exercise due to the time investment that the player has put in, but there are 2 points to consider.

The pacing of this game is much slower than one might be accustomed to. Non-action moments are indeed very slow - loading in to a match, traversing portions of the map, etc. However, this does build anticipation. Action moments are very action packed and the pacing rises right up there with Destiny Trials or any other intense high stress moments that you may have experienced. To put it another way, the highs are high and the lows are low (but not that low once you get used to it).

Second, Bungie does an excellent job of alleviating the gear fear pain point through the introduction of the Rook class. The only other extraction shooter that I have experience with is Arc Raiders so I can't say if they all implement this approach, but I do know that Arc does not and Marathon does. The Rook class loads into a game-in-progress fairly late into the game (~10 min left). Many players have already died and their loot is ripe for the taking. Many threats still exist though so this is not a freebie and still requires calculated gameplay. There are other teams that are still alive as well as other Rooks that have loaded in. The Rook has a special ability to "blend in" with UESC forces that prevents them (the AI, not other players) from attacking. In this way the Rook can traverse through hostile zones without engaging in combat, allowing it to get to hard to reach places full of gear. This allows runs that can seemingly quickly populate a player's inventory with good gear.

All of this adds up to what I think is a very strict economy (hard to make money and things are expensive) but highly dynamic (high turnover). There will be big losses of gear but the Rook class really promotes replacement of that gear in a good way. Don't get me wrong though, Rook runs are very challenging and carry a lot of weight. The probability of dying is still very high. But the good thing is that it can ultimately boil down to just "play the game".

Treated in this way, the game opens up to be competitive PvP matches. Outside of the Rook runs, players use their specialized team shells to interact with the game world as it's intended to be interacted with - raiding, fighting other players, completing contracts, leveling up (new abilities to bring into combat). "For what?" Well for nothing. To play a PvP game and have some fun and memorable moments. We are no longer carrying the gear fear baggage and instead are able to focus on the game itself presently. And this is where Marathon shines. The Bungie physics engine, the PvP strategies and tactics and quick decision making in the moment to gain an advantage for you and your team and ultimately win fights. The world building and story telling. All of the things that have made past Bungie titles hits!
