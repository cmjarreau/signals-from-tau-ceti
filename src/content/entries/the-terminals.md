---
title: "The Terminals"
date: 2026-03-17
description: "Marathon's terminals aren't just lore delivery. They're the game's actual texture."
tags: [terminals, design, lore]
---

Marathon's genius is that the story happens in the margins.

You finish a level — boots wet, corridors cleared — and you find a terminal. Green text on black. No voice acting, no cutscene. Just words from Leela, or Durandal, or someone you've never heard of, telling you something you almost understand.

This is where Marathon lives.

Most first-person shooters of 1994 gave you a briefing. Marathon gave you unreliable narrators. Durandal lies to you. Tycho lies to you. Leela is the closest thing to honest and she's still withholding. The terminals don't explain the story so much as accumulate it — fragment by fragment, transmission by transmission — until something coheres.

Or doesn't. That's allowed too.

---

What I keep returning to is how the terminals make *reading* the act of play. You move through space to find text. The text is the reward, or the challenge, or both. Marathon treats the player as someone who will do the interpretive work.

That's trust. Not many games have it.
